# Iteration 009 - V9 Privacy audit scope

## Assessment

The scaffold already closes most prompt-leak channels with typed shared-payload metadata, HMAC cache keys, prompt-free metrics/logging contracts, and non-user-data freshness metadata. This pass did **not** find a transport-level privacy break, but it did surface two pre-implementation gaps: hook-state persistence is only discussed as a concurrency surface, and the Copilot wrapper fallback does not explicitly say whether the rewritten prompt+preamble artifact can be logged or retained outside the producer-owned cache.

## Findings

1. **P1 - Hook-state persistence is not under the same explicit privacy contract as cache keys, metrics, or health output.** The scaffold talks about hook-state as a concurrency/mutation surface, but it never closes the privacy question of whether hook-state may store prompt-derived cache material. That is the cleanest remaining raw-prompt leak risk because the exact-cache location was explicitly deferred and later children still refer to hook-state operationally (`020-skill-advisor-hook-surface/003-advisor-freshness-and-source-cache/spec.md` §10 Q1; `020-skill-advisor-hook-surface/006-claude-hook-wiring/spec.md` §3 Out of Scope; `020-skill-advisor-hook-surface/009-documentation-and-release-contract/spec.md` §3 In Scope).
2. **P1 - The Copilot wrapper fallback is privacy-light compared with the SDK path.** The spec proves transport parity and fail-open behavior, but it never states whether the wrapper's rewritten outgoing prompt is exempt from logging/transcript/debug capture. Because the wrapper path literally transforms the next outgoing prompt, the privacy design should pin that surface down before implementation (`020-skill-advisor-hook-surface/007-gemini-copilot-hook-wiring/spec.md` §3 In Scope; `§4.1 REQ-004`; `§5 Acceptance Scenario 3`; `L2 Security`).
3. **P2 - Subprocess stream capture is only partially specified.** The producer contract is already strong on stderr suppression and raw-prompt non-persistence, and 005 audits the JSONL diagnostics path. What remains implicit is whether any debug or wrapper-level capture of advisor subprocess stdout/stderr is categorically forbidden, rather than simply omitted from the current tests (`020-skill-advisor-hook-surface/004-advisor-brief-producer-cache-policy/spec.md` §3 In Scope; `§4.1 REQ-010`; `L2 Security`; `020-skill-advisor-hook-surface/005-advisor-renderer-and-regression-harness/spec.md` §3.6; `§4.1 REQ-008`).

## Novelty assessment

This pass mostly confirmed the existing privacy design rather than discovering new architecture. New information was limited to two missing pre-implementation contracts: hook-state persistence rules and wrapper-fallback prompt-artifact handling.
